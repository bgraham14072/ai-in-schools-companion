Introduction: 
A Journey into Transformative Education by Brian S. Graham, Ed.D. 
In the early years of my career as a special education teacher, I was 
fortunate to work under the guidance of a principal whose leadership was 
nothing short of visionary. At Alden Central Schools, Mr. Tom Lyons 
embodied the spirit of innovation, placing immense trust in his teachers 
and empowering us to experiment with tools and strategies that could 
unlock the potential of every student. His unwavering belief in the power 
of education to transform lives—and his willingness to back that belief with 
action—became the cornerstone of my professional journey. 
One of the most impactful moments in those early years was the 
introduction of the Kurzweil Reading Machine, a groundbreaking invention 
by Ray Kurzweil. At a time when assistive technology was still in its 
infancy, this device revolutionized the way we approached literacy for 
students with disabilities. The machine transformed printed text into 
spoken words, breaking down barriers that had previously excluded 
students from accessing grade-level materials. It was more than a tool; it 
was a lifeline for countless children who had struggled to connect with the 
written word. 
Building on this success, Tom’s leadership extended to the adoption of 
Dragon NaturallySpeaking in the late 1990s. For many of my students, this 
speech recognition software was nothing short of miraculous. Suddenly, 
children with dyslexia, motor impairments, or other challenges could 
articulate their thoughts in writing with accuracy and fluency. I still recall 
one young boy whose world seemed to open up when he could "write" by 
simply speaking. His writing—once a source of frustration and tears—
became a canvas for creativity and self-expression. Watching him 
experience the joy of being understood was a powerful reminder of 
technology’s potential to level the playing field. 
These experiences weren’t confined to literacy alone. Technology became 
a bridge for students with profound physical and intellectual disabilities. 
Two young children in my classroom, each facing significant challenges, 
taught me profound lessons about resilience and the transformative power 
of innovation. One student, who had severe cerebral palsy, demonstrated 
remarkable progress using a custom-built interface that combined an 
infrared beam-of-light switch and a Macintosh program called HyperStudio. 
By moving his pinky finger through the beam of light, he could navigate a 
yes/no interface we designed together, answering questions with an 
astonishing 70% accuracy. For a child who had previously been unable to 
communicate his thoughts, this achievement was revolutionary.

Chapter 1: The AI Revolution in Education: A Historical Perspective 
and Foundational Understanding by Brian S. Graham, Ed.D 
The integration of technology into public school classrooms has been a 
transformative journey spanning over a century, continually evolving to 
meet the changing needs of education. This journey, from basic tools to 
advanced AI, reflects a persistent commitment to improving accessibility, 
efficiency, engagement, and equity in learning. Each technological 
milestone has not only introduced new tools but also shifted the very 
dynamics of teaching and learning, redefining the roles of educators and 
students alike. Just as the invention of the printing press by Johannes 
Gutenberg in the 15th century revolutionized access to knowledge, 
dismantling barriers that had long kept learning in the hands of the few, 
today’s artificial intelligence tools are poised to democratize education 
once again. The printing press empowered educators, scholars, and 
ordinary citizens to engage with ideas in ways previously unimaginable. 
Similarly, AI has the potential to reshape how we teach, learn, and lead—
bringing personalized learning, real-time support, and dynamic content 
creation into every classroom. 
A Century of Educational Innovation 
Beginning with radios in the 1920s, technology expanded access to 
learning by bringing on-air lessons into classrooms, centralizing knowledge 
delivery and allowing for more standardized content. Early innovations 
such as filmstrips, overhead projectors, and videotapes provided 
engaging visual aids that enhanced traditional teaching methods. 
Filmstrips allowed for sequential visual narratives, while overheads 
enabled real-time, shared visual explanations without erasing, beginning 
to shift classroom dynamics from purely textbook-and-chalkboard to a 
more multimodal experience. The introduction of the ditto machine in the 
mid-20th century marked another pivotal moment in educational access—
bringing the spirit of Gutenberg’s printing revolution into everyday 
classrooms. Where the printing press had made books widely available, 
the ditto machine gave teachers the power to rapidly duplicate lessons, 
worksheets, and newsletters, personalizing learning materials at scale. 
This democratization of printed content enabled students to engage with 
learning on a more individualized basis, supporting varied pacing and 
differentiation long before the term became part of the educational 
lexicon. 

17 

By the 1970s and 1980s, office technologies like printers, copiers, and 
fax machines began to significantly enhance both instructional efficiency 
and administrative workflows. The arrival of photocopiers only amplified 
this capacity, making high-volume reproduction of educational content 
both quicker and more accessible. These innovations—once mechanical, 
now digital—paved the way for today’s AI-powered tools, which not only 
duplicate content but dynamically generate and adapt it in real time. 
Where Gutenberg broke the monopoly on knowledge, and the copier 
brought it to the hands of every student, AI now offers the promise to 
tailor it to each learner—one query, one response, one need at a time. Fax 
machines enabled rapid document sharing between schools, districts, and 
parents during an era before widespread internet access. The 1980s also 
marked the rise of personal computers (PCs) in education, introducing 
interactive learning experiences that revolutionized how students engaged 
with content. Early educational software, often drill-and-practice, began to 
introduce the concept of adaptive learning, where students could progress 
at their own pace. The graphical interface of Apple Macintosh, 
democratized computer use, making it accessible even to younger 
students and fostering creativity through early desktop publishing and 
graphic design applications. CD-ROMs in the mid-1980s allowed for 
extensive multimedia libraries to be stored and accessed easily, further 
enriching learning resources. 
The 1990s saw the emergence of the internet in schools, fostering a 
paradigm shift from isolated knowledge centers to nodes within a global 
information network. This enabled new forms of collaborative learning and 
project-based inquiry, blurring the lines between school and the wider 
world. The E-Rate Program in 1996 subsidized internet access, 
promoting greater equity. SMART Boards, introduced in 1999, 
transformed classrooms into interactive digital learning environments, 
facilitating direct annotation, saving of lesson content, and new 
opportunities for interactive group problem-solving. By the late 1990s, 
VOIP phones further revolutionized communication within schools by 
reducing costs and improving connectivity between educators and 
administrators. 
The 2000s ushered in accelerated digital transformation with widespread 
internet access and the emergence of Learning Management Systems 
(LMS) like Moodle and Blackboard. These platforms centralized course 
content and began to collect student interaction data, laying early 
groundwork for insights into learning patterns. The introduction of the 
iPhone in 2007 paved the way for widespread mobile learning initiatives. 

18 

The 2010s saw tablets (notably iPads) and Chromebooks become 
mainstream in classrooms, enhancing individualized instruction and 
providing affordable solutions for one-to-one computing initiatives. The 
ubiquity of these mobile devices truly empowered "anytime, anywhere" 
learning, blurring the boundaries between formal classroom instruction 
and informal, self-directed exploration. The launch of Google Classroom 
streamlined digital communication, assignment distribution, and 
collaboration, while the growth of Open Educational Resources (OER) 
democratized access to high-quality educational materials online. 
Today, advanced technologies like AI-driven adaptive platforms and 
immersive virtual reality (VR) experiences are reshaping education. 
These tools empower teachers to tailor instruction to individual student 
needs while fostering engagement through interactive content. AI-powered 
assistants like Khanmigo and iReady provide personalized tutoring for 
students while supporting teachers with data analysis and differentiated 
instruction. VR and augmented reality (AR) offer experiential learning 
opportunities through virtual field trips and simulations that bring abstract 
concepts to life.

What AI Is in Education 
Artificial Intelligence (AI) in education refers to the integration of 
technologies capable of mimicking human intelligence to enhance 
teaching, learning, and administrative processes. In this context, AI is not 
a replacement for educators but a tool that supports and augments 
educational practices. 
AI is transforming education by offering a range of capabilities that 
enhance both teaching and learning. One of its most significant 
contributions is personalization, as AI enables tailored learning 
experiences by adapting content to individual student needs, learning 
styles, and progress. 
Adaptive learning platforms like Century Tech and Khanmigo analyze 
student performance data to create customized pathways and provide 
immediate feedback, ensuring that each student receives targeted 
support. Additionally, AI excels in automation, streamlining routine tasks 
such as grading, scheduling, and administrative management. Tools like 
Gradescope simplify grading processes, while AI-driven chatbots offer 24/7 
tutoring support, giving students access to assistance whenever they need 
it. 

27 

Beyond personalization and automation, AI provides data-driven 
insights by analyzing vast amounts of educational data to identify trends, 
predict outcomes such as dropout risks, and inform instructional 
strategies. These insights empower educators to make informed decisions 
that improve student outcomes. AI also serves as a powerful tool for 
assistive technology, supporting students with disabilities through 
innovations like speech recognition software and platforms that adapt 
content for neurodiverse learners, ensuring equitable access to education. 
Furthermore, AI enhances interactive learning by creating engaging 
experiences through virtual labs, educational games, and intelligent 
tutoring systems that deepen conceptual understanding. Together, these 
capabilities demonstrate the transformative potential of AI in fostering 
more effective and inclusive educational environments. 

What AI Is Not: The Indispensable Role of Human Educators 
While AI offers valuable support for instructional tasks, it is not a 
replacement for teachers. AI lacks the emotional intelligence, creativity, 
and real-time adaptability that human educators bring to the classroom. 
Teachers remain essential for fostering critical thinking, motivating 
students, and providing mentorship—roles that go beyond what AI can 
achieve. Far from replacing educators, AI redefines their roles, enabling 
them to shift focus from transactional tasks (like grading drills) to 
transformational ones. This includes fostering deeper student 
relationships, guiding complex inquiry, nurturing social-emotional 
development, and cultivating essential human skills such as critical 
thinking, creativity, and empathy, which remain uniquely human domains. 
Additionally, AI systems are not perfect or entirely unbiased. They are only 
as effective as the data they are trained on, and biases in that data can 
lead to unfair outcomes or misinformation, underscoring the need for 
careful oversight and ethical implementation. The responsible integration 
of AI necessitates a focus on digital literacy. Students must be equipped 
with the critical thinking skills to evaluate AI-generated information, 
understand algorithmic biases, and navigate the ethical implications of 
data privacy in an AI-powered world. 

28 

AI also cannot substitute for the human interaction that is central to 
education. Social and emotional connections between teachers and 
students are vital for creating supportive learning environments, and over-
reliance on AI risks diminishing these important relationships. Finally, 
while AI excels at automating tasks and offering solutions based on 
existing patterns, it struggles with creative problem-solving or tasks 
requiring out-of-the-box thinking. These limitations highlight the 
complementary role of AI in education rather than its ability to replace the 
unique contributions of human educators. 

Summary of Impact: The New Frontier 
This timeline underscores technology’s ongoing role as an accelerator of 
educational transformation. From the hornbooks and chalkboards of early 
American schools to today’s sophisticated AI tutors and immersive AR/VR 
environments, each step has significantly expanded teachers' pedagogical 
possibilities, increased student engagement, and advanced equitable 
access to education. Today, artificial intelligence represents a new frontier, 
combining historical lessons with cutting-edge innovation to redefine

## Understanding Historiography and Reconstruction 

### Introduction to Historiography 
Historiography is the study of how historians interpret historical events 
over time. It's like being a detective, trying to figure out not just what 
happened in the past, but also why different people have different ideas 
about those events. Understanding historiography helps us see that history 
isn't just a set of facts; it's a story that can be told in many ways. 

### Overview of Reconstruction 
Reconstruction refers to the period after the American Civil War (1861-
1865) when the United States tried to rebuild and redefine itself. This era, 
roughly from 1865 to 1877, was crucial because it dealt with how to 
integrate the Southern states back into the Union and what rights to give 
to formerly enslaved individuals. Reconstruction is significant within Early 
Modern History (a period that generally refers to the time between the late 
15th century and the late 18th or early 19th century, though 
Reconstruction itself is more accurately placed in the late 19th century, it 
can be connected to broader themes in Early Modern History such as the 
development of modern nation-states and the struggle for civil rights) 
because it shows the challenges of creating a more equal society after a 
major conflict. 

### Changing Interpretations of Reconstruction 
Let's explore how historians' views on Reconstruction's impact and 
consequences have changed over time: 

1. Early Interpretation ( Late 19th-Early 20th Century) 
 - Time Period: Late 19th to Early 20th Century 
 - Key Historians: Historians like William A. Dunning 
 - Main Arguments: They saw Reconstruction as a failure and a period of 
corruption and misrule, especially in the South. They believed that the 
North's attempts to impose its will on the South were misguided and that 
the South's resistance was justified. 

35 

 - Influencing Factors: These historians were influenced by the racial 
attitudes of their time, which were often prejudiced against African 
Americans. 

2. Revisionist Interpretation (Mid-20th Century) 
 - Time Period: Mid-20th Century 
 - Key Historians: Historians like W.E.B. Du Bois and Kenneth Stampp 
 - Main Arguments: They argued that Reconstruction was a noble effort 
to create a more equal society, but it was thwarted by white resistance in 
the South and lack of sustained support from the North. 
 - Influencing Factors: The Civil Rights Movement of the 1950s and 1960s 
influenced these historians, making them more sympathetic to the goals of 
Reconstruction. 

3. Post-Revisionist Interpretation (Late 20th Century) 
 - Time Period: Late 20th Century 
 - Key Historians: Historians like Eric Foner 
 - Main Arguments: They emphasized the complexity of Reconstruction, 
highlighting both its achievements (like the establishment of the 
Freedmen's Bureau and the passage of important legislation) and its 
limitations (such as the failure to achieve lasting economic equality for 
African Americans). 
 - Influencing Factors: Advances in social and cultural history, as well as 
a more nuanced understanding of the experiences of different groups 
during Reconstruction. 

### Factors Influencing Changing Interpretations 
- New Sources and Evidence: The discovery of new documents and records 
has allowed historians to reevaluate Reconstruction. 
- Advances in Research Methodologies: Techniques like quantitative 
analysis and oral history have provided new insights. 
- Cultural and Societal Changes: The Civil Rights Movement and changing 
racial attitudes have influenced historians' perspectives. 

36 

- Interdisciplinary Approaches: Incorporating insights from sociology, 
anthropology, and other fields has enriched historical understanding. 

### Current Debates 
Historians continue to debate the extent of Reconstruction's successes and 
failures, and how to balance the achievements of the period with its 
limitations. 

### Impact on Understanding Early Modern History 
The changing interpretations of Reconstruction remind us that history is 
not static. They show how the context in which historians write influences 
their conclusions, reflecting broader societal changes and advances in 
historical methodology. 

### Critical Thinking and Multiple Perspectives 
Studying historiography encourages critical thinking. It teaches us to 
question historical accounts and consider multiple viewpoints, recognizing 
that history is a complex and multifaceted story. 

### Approaching Historical Sources Critically 
- Questions to Ask: Who wrote this? When? Why? What biases might they 
have had? 
- Identifying Biases: Look for loaded language, selective use of evidence, 
and omission of certain viewpoints.

2. Debate: Hold a debate on the successes and failures of Reconstruction, 
using different historians' interpretations as sources. 
3. Source Analysis: Analyze primary sources from Reconstruction, 
discussing how they support or challenge different historical

(pages 49-58) by Brian S. Graham, Ed.D.

Chapter 3 - Ethical Considerations and Challenges by Brian S. 
Graham, Ed.D 

Framing the Conversation: Why Ethics Matter Now 
As artificial intelligence becomes increasingly embedded in our schools, the 
conversation can no longer center on if we use AI—but how we do so 
responsibly. While AI holds tremendous promise for transforming 
instruction, supporting teachers, and increasing equity, it also raises 
urgent questions about data privacy, algorithmic bias, access, and 
the shifting role of educators. Just as the internet necessitated new 
discussions around digital citizenship and cyberbullying, the unprecedented 
capabilities of AI demand a more nuanced and proactive ethical 
framework. This isn't merely about compliance; it's about safeguarding the 
core values of education itself. 
The rapid evolution of AI technology means that ethical frameworks cannot 
be static. We must build dynamic systems that can adapt as quickly as AI 
itself, ensuring our policies remain relevant and protective. If we fail to 
address these ethical considerations head-on, we risk undermining trust, 
widening opportunity gaps, and reinforcing systemic biases. But if we lead 
with intention—placing humanity, inclusion, and pedagogy at the center—
we can harness AI to create safer, fairer, and more empowering learning 
environments for all. 

Data Privacy: Protecting Student Rights in a Digital Age 
One of the most pressing ethical concerns with AI in education is data 
privacy. AI tools—particularly adaptive learning platforms and generative 
models—rely on large volumes of student data to personalize instruction, 
recommend content, or analyze trends. But what safeguards are in place 
to ensure that data is protected, not exploited? 
Key considerations include: 
• Ownership: Who owns the data generated by student interactions 
with AI tools? Districts must establish clear contracts with vendors 
to retain ownership and control over student information, ensuring 
it's not used for commercial purposes or secondary analyses 
without explicit permission. 

50 

• Consent and Transparency: Students and families should know 
what data is being collected, how it’s used, and with whom it’s 
shared. Consent should be ongoing, not a one-time checkbox, 
allowing families to make informed decisions about their child's 
digital footprint. 
• Security Protocols: As cyber threats grow, districts must ensure 
that AI vendors comply with the highest standards of encryption, 
access control, and secure storage. Beyond encryption, districts 
should inquire about data de-identification and anonymization 
practices, ensuring that individual student data cannot be easily 
traced back to them when used for aggregate analysis or model 
training. 
• Data Minimization: AI tools should only collect the data 
absolutely necessary for their intended educational purpose, 
avoiding extraneous information that could pose unnecessary 
privacy risks. 
We must also prepare for scenarios that don’t yet exist: What happens 
when an AI tool predicts a student's likelihood of failing a course—or 
dropping out? How do we prevent that information from shaping 
expectations in harmful ways? The ethical tightrope here involves 
balancing the potential benefits of early intervention with the risks of 
algorithmic determinism. Such predictions, if not handled with extreme 
care and human oversight, could lead to self-fulfilling prophecies, reinforce 
stereotypes, or unfairly categorize students based on data that might not 
capture their full potential or circumstances. It's crucial to ensure these 
insights empower, rather than label, educators and students. 
Ultimately, privacy policies must be future-ready, regularly audited, and 
co-developed with all stakeholders, including parents, legal experts, and 
students themselves. 

Equity and Access: Avoiding a New Digital Divide 
AI has the potential to close learning gaps—but it can also exacerbate 
them if access is unequal or if tools are designed without diverse learners 
in mind. 
Equity concerns fall into two categories: 

51 

• Access to Technology: Students in under-resourced schools may 
lack consistent internet access, modern devices, or the digital 
literacy needed to benefit from AI-enhanced learning. Districts 
must address these disparities through investments in 
infrastructure, community partnerships, and mobile-first or offline-
compatible tools to ensure no student is left behind. 
• Bias in AI Models: AI systems are only as fair as the data they 
are trained on. If historical data reflects existing inequities—based 
on race, gender, disability, or language—AI outputs may reinforce 
those same patterns. For example, an AI tool might misidentify a 
student’s writing as "off-topic" due to cultural references it wasn’t 
trained to understand. Similarly, an AI tutor designed for 
"standard" English might struggle to understand diverse accents or 
dialects, inadvertently penalizing students whose linguistic 
backgrounds differ from the training data. Or, an adaptive platform 
might inadvertently offer less engaging content to students from 
lower socioeconomic backgrounds if its algorithms correlate certain 
data points with disengagement. It's also important to consider the 
intersectionality of bias, where students belonging to multiple 
marginalized groups (e.g., a non-native English speaker with a 
learning disability from a low-income family) may face 
compounded disadvantages if AI systems are not meticulously 
designed and audited for multifaceted equity. 
To combat this, educational leaders must: 
• Choose vendors that prioritize inclusive datasets and robust bias 
auditing, demanding transparency in their model development. 
• Regularly review outputs for unintended consequences, actively 
seeking feedback from diverse student populations. 
• Involve students and families from diverse backgrounds in user-
testing and feedback loops, ensuring tools are truly effective and 
equitable for all. 
• Actively curate and supplement AI-generated content with diverse, 
culturally relevant resources that reflect the rich tapestry of 
student experiences, compensating for potential gaps in AI training 
data. 
Equity cannot be an afterthought. It must be designed into the AI lifecycle 
from procurement to classroom implementation. 

52 

The Human Element: Balancing Technology with Pedagogy 
Even as AI becomes more sophisticated, it cannot replace the heart of 
teaching: the human connection between educators and students. The 
danger is not that AI will replace teachers—but that it might lead us to 
undervalue the intuition, empathy, and creativity that only humans bring 
to education. The beauty of AI lies in its ability to automate repetitive 
tasks and crunch vast datasets, thereby liberating educators to focus on 
precisely what AI cannot do: cultivate genuine human connection, foster 
social-emotional growth, inspire critical thinking through nuanced 
dialogue, and provide the deep, individualized mentorship that transcends 
algorithmic suggestions. 
Educators must remain at the center of AI implementation: 
• Teachers as Decision-Makers: AI should inform—not dictate—
instructional decisions. When an AI tool suggests a learning 
pathway, teachers must still consider the whole child, their unique 
circumstances, and their holistic development. 
• Professional Judgment: An algorithm might flag a student as 
"struggling," but a teacher may know that the student just lost a 
loved one or changed schools. Context matters, and a teacher's 
lived experience with their students is irreplaceable. An ethical 
consideration for educators is the risk of deskilling or over-
reliance on AI for tasks that still require professional judgment. 
While AI can draft a lesson plan, the teacher's expertise in 
understanding classroom dynamics, individual student 
personalities, and real-time engagement is paramount to its 
successful implementation. 
• Relationship Building: Trust, motivation, and social-emotional 
learning flourish in human interactions. AI can support these 
dynamics by streamlining administrative tasks or providing quick 
feedback, but it can never replace the bond formed between a 
student and their teacher. 
One of the most ethical decisions we can make is to position AI as a 
teaching resource, not a replacement. Used well, AI enhances pedagogical 
artistry—it does not erase it. When positioned strategically, AI serves as a 
powerful cognitive enhancer for educators, providing new insights and 
efficiencies that amplify their pedagogical artistry rather than erasing it. 

53 

Putting Ethics into Practice: The UNESCO AI Competency 
Frameworks 
The challenges of privacy, equity, and pedagogy are not insurmountable. 
They require a structured, intentional approach to building capacity. 
Recognizing this need, UNESCO launched its AI Competency Frameworks 
for students and teachers in September 2024, providing a global blueprint 
for integrating AI into education ethically and effectively. These 
frameworks move the conversation from identifying problems to actively 
building solutions. 
Grounded in a human-centered approach, the frameworks reject passive 
AI consumption, instead framing students as co-creators and teachers as 
ethical stewards. They offer a clear roadmap for what students should 
know and what teachers must be able to do. 
A Competency Roadmap for Students: UNESCO's framework for 
students is built on four key dimensions, with learners progressing through 
three levels of mastery: Understand → Apply → Create. The goal is to 
weave these competencies into existing subjects rather than siloing them 
in a separate AI course: 
1. Human-centered mindset: Developing agency and a critical 
awareness of AI's societal impact. 
2. Ethics of AI: Understanding responsible use, bias mitigation, and 
ethics-by-design—directly addressing the privacy and equity 
concerns outlined earlier. 
3. AI techniques and applications: Gaining foundational 
knowledge of how AI systems work. 
4. AI system design: Fostering creative and ethical problem-solving 
using AI tools. 
Empowering Teachers as Ethical Stewards: The framework for 
teachers is organized across five competency areas, designed to ensure 
educators can lead AI integration responsibly: 
1. Human-centered mindset: Prioritizing accountability and social 
responsibility in AI use. 
2. Ethics of AI: Implementing ethical guidelines and navigating 
dilemmas in classroom settings. 
3. AI foundations and applications: Achieving technical 
proficiency with educational AI tools. 

54 

4. AI pedagogy: Innovating teaching methods to purposefully 
integrate AI in a way that enhances learning, not just technology 
for technology's sake. 
5. AI for professional development: Leveraging AI for their own 
lifelong learning and continuous improvement. 
These frameworks provide an invaluable, authoritative guide for any 
school system serious about its ethical obligations. They transform 
abstract principles into concrete, teachable skills. 

Creating a Culture of Responsible AI Use 
Adopting a powerful model like the UNESCO frameworks is not a top-down 
mandate; it requires cultivating a supportive and reflective school culture. 
To operationalize such a vision, leaders must lay the groundwork by: 
• Establishing Ethics Committees or Advisory Councils: Form 
multidisciplinary teams that review AI tools and policies before 
adoption, ensuring broad input from educators, administrators, 
parents, legal experts, and community members. This directly 
supports the implementation of the "Ethics of AI" competency. 
• Developing Clear Guidelines and Communication: Publish AI 
usage expectations and protocols. Ensure comprehensive 
communication with families about AI's role, benefits, and 
safeguards, fostering an informed partnership. 
• Centering Student Voices: Invite students to share their 
experiences with AI tools. Do they trust the tool? Do they feel seen 
and understood by it? These qualitative insights are crucial for 
ensuring a truly human-centered approach. 
• Investing in Ongoing Professional Learning: A culture of 
responsible AI use thrives on continuous dialogue. This means 
providing regular workshops and dedicated time for educators to 
reflect on their experiences—directly fulfilling the "AI for 
professional development" competency and strengthening their 
capacity in "AI pedagogy." 

55 

To bring this competency-based roadmap to life, educational systems must 
create a supportive ecosystem of policy, professional learning, and 
partnership. The call for local ethics committees or advisory councils—
comprising educators, administrators, parents, and community members—
directly aligns with UNESCO's recommendation for member states to 
engage in multi-stakeholder cooperation and develop national AI 
strategies.10 These committees can review potential AI tools and establish 
clear guidelines for their use, ensuring that procurement and 
implementation are aligned with community values. The frameworks are 
explicitly designed to "guide the development of national AI competency 
frameworks," and local efforts are the essential first step in this process.5 
Perhaps the most critical enabler is ongoing professional learning and 
dialogue. UNESCO explicitly identifies the lack of teacher-training 
programs as a primary barrier to ethical AI adoption and designed the 
frameworks specifically to "inform teacher training programmes".5 This 
underscores a necessary evolution in professional development. The 
traditional model of one-off technology workshops focused on how to use a 
specific tool is no longer adequate. The UNESCO Teacher Framework, with 
its advanced dimensions of "AI Pedagogy" and "AI for professional 
learning" and its "Acquire → Deepen → Create" progression, demands a 
far more sophisticated approach. It calls for continuous, pedagogy-focused 
professional learning that equips educators to become lifelong learners 
themselves—capable of critically evaluating any new tool and adapting 
their practice accordingly. This is a complex, sustained endeavor that 
requires dedicated time, resources, and institutional commitment. 
This commitment must be realistic. UNESCO issues a critical caution that 
AI is not a "quick fix" for systemic challenges and that successful 
implementation requires "sustained policy attention and investment" in 
human capacity and infrastructure.14 This reality check is vital for leaders 
planning AI initiatives. Finally, these efforts do not need to happen in a 
vacuum. The UNESCO frameworks were designed to complement existing 
international standards, such as Europe’s DigiCompEdu and DigComp 2.2, 
demonstrating that this work is part of a coherent global movement 
toward deeper digital literacy.7 

56 

When these system-level enablers are put in place, a powerful feedback 
loop can be created between local practice and global policy. UNESCO 
makes it clear that its frameworks are a "global reference" and encourages 
nations to "tailor the frameworks to local needs." This implies a dynamic, 
two-way relationship. As schools and districts establish ethics committees, 
engage in professional learning, and gather feedback from students and 
families, they are not just passively implementing top-down policy. They 
are actively generating crucial, ground-level data and qualitative evidence 
about what works, what fails, and what unintended consequences arise in 
diverse contexts. This local knowledge is invaluable. It can inform the 
refinement of regional and national strategies, and ultimately, it can be fed 
back to the international community to inform future iterations of global 
guidance. In this model, the local school becomes a vital laboratory for 
shaping a more ethical global future for AI in education. 
Conclusion: Charting an Ethical Future for AI in Education 
The integration of artificial intelligence into education represents a 
watershed moment, one that holds the dual potential to either deepen 
inequities and undermine human connection or to unlock unprecedented 
opportunities for personalized, engaging, and empowering learning. The 
central ethical challenge of our time is not to block the advance of this 
technology, but to steer it with intention, wisdom, and a steadfast 
commitment to humanistic values.3 This requires moving beyond a state of 
reactive anxiety toward one of proactive and purposeful agency. 
The UNESCO AI Competency Frameworks for students and teachers, 
launched in the fall of 2024, provide the essential global compass for this 
journey. For the first time, the international education community has a 
shared language and a structured, comprehensive approach to what was 
previously a fragmented, and often chaotic, conversation. The frameworks 
transform abstract ethical principles into tangible competencies, providing 
a clear roadmap for what it means to be a responsible user, a critical 
thinker, and an ethical designer in the age of AI. They offer a powerful 
rebuttal to technological determinism, asserting that the future of AI in 
education is not something that will simply happen to us; it is something 
that we must actively and collectively build. 

57 

However, these frameworks are not a final destination or a rigid 
prescription to be passively adopted. They are, above all, a call to action.14 
They challenge educators, school leaders, policymakers, and technology 
developers to engage in the difficult but necessary work of co-creating an 
educational future where technology serves pedagogy, not the other way 
around. This work demands the establishment of new collaborative 
structures, a deep commitment to ongoing professional learning, and an 
unwavering focus on the most vulnerable learners. It requires us to ask 
hard questions about data, to confront bias head-on, and to always 
prioritize the human element that lies at the heart of all meaningful 
education. 
By placing the human-centered principles and concrete competencies 
outlined by UNESCO at the core of our strategies, we can harness the 
power of AI to create learning environments that are not only more 
efficient and innovative but also more just, equitable, and profoundly 
human. The task is to ensure that as our tools become smarter, we as 
educators and as a society become wiser.

Chapter 4 - Putting the "Human" Back in Human Resources - AI in 
District Administration by John Fitzpatrick 

As an Assistant Superintendent for Human Resources, I have come to 
believe that one of the greatest opportunities artificial intelligence offers 
school leaders is the ability to put the “human” back into human 
resources. Too often, our roles are consumed by compliance, contracts, 
and procedural minutiae. It’s important work, but work that often pulls us 
away from the people we are here to support. AI has changed that for me. 
In my experience, AI is not a replacement for human judgment, 
leadership, or empathy. Rather, it is a powerful assistant, a thought 
partner, that helps me navigate complexity more efficiently, so I can 
redirect my energy toward the heart of this profession: our people. With 
the time AI saves, I’m able to focus more intently on understanding each 
employee’s strengths, challenges, and motivations. This has allowed me to 
lead more personally, to coach more intentionally, and to empower more 
effectively. 
A leader’s priority is not to micromanage tasks, but to inspire growth that 
leads to transformation. AI allows us to shift our focus from management 
to leadership, from tasks that are transactional to work that is 
transformational. But that shift only happens when we approach AI not as 
a decision-maker, but as a decision-support tool. The leader must provide 
direction; AI plays the role of a partner, helping us clarify, draft, calculate, 
and synthesize. In doing so, we can lead with greater clarity and capacity. 
The following case studies illustrate how I have used AI tools to support 
my work in human resources. Each example shows a real use case, rooted 
in contracts, benefits, evaluations, or compliance, and how AI has made 
these responsibilities more manageable, more accurate, and more human-
centered. These are not abstract hypotheticals. They are practical, 
repeatable strategies that any school leader can use to better serve their 
people and move their district forward. 
These case studies are organized around key areas of human resources 
where AI has proven especially helpful in my daily work. Each one 
highlights how AI supported a specific challenge, enhanced efficiency, and 
ultimately allowed for more personal, values-driven leadership. 

60 

To maximize the effectiveness of AI in my role, I uploaded each of our 
collective bargaining unit contracts into the platform. This allowed me to 
quickly reference contractual language, input detailed employee scenarios, 
and explore possible solutions and outcomes with precision. By providing 
context-rich queries, I could engage AI not just as a tool for retrieving 
information, but as a collaborator in weighing potential decisions for 
equity, legality, and alignment with both New York State and federal 
education and labor laws. As the thought leader, I rely on AI to present 
perspectives and streamline processes, never to replace human judgment, 
but to sharpen it. 

Case Study 1: Clarifying Severance Benefits and Retirement Filing 
Timelines 
Retirement is a deeply personal milestone for every employee, and 
managing the details behind it requires both precision and compassion. In 
one case, I was asked to determine the severance benefit for an employee 
with a specific number of unused sick days and no personal days, along 
with an annual salary. Using AI, I was able to quickly apply contract 
language to calculate the payout and verify adherence to the contractual 
cap. 
The initial question led to several follow-ups: What if the employee had 
280 sick days instead of 190? Does the contract limit how many can be 
cashed out? What if they filed for retirement on July 1 instead of June 30? 
With the contracts already uploaded, I could present AI with detailed 
"what-if" scenarios and receive tailored responses that considered both the 
collective bargaining agreement and relevant NYS retirement system 
guidelines. 
AI helped me refine severance letters, calculate dollar values down to the 
day, and ensure the tone of communications reflected respect and 
gratitude. It also enabled me to anticipate employee concerns and prepare 
responses that were both fair and compliant. 
That said, it's important to note that AI outputs always require a follow-up 
review. Occasionally, the platform’s math needed adjusting as it may have 
been inaccurate by pennies, or the cited contract page didn’t align 
perfectly with our local agreements. To ensure accuracy, I also uploaded 
reference materials from the New York State Teachers’ Retirement System 
(NYSTRS) and used them alongside the collective bargaining agreements. 
AI served as my starting point, a powerful guide, but it was never the final 
word. The key is knowing how to ask the right questions and always 
checking your work before making final decisions that affect people’s lives.

Case Study 2: Managing Qualifying Events and Coverage Continuity 
During Retirement Transitions 
Health insurance coverage during retirement transitions can be one of the 
most stressful concerns for employees and their families. Ensuring that 
there are no gaps in coverage, and that employees understand their 
options, requires both technical knowledge and clear, empathetic

enroll in her own coverage after her husband, the primary policyholder, 
retired and lost his district-provided insurance. This seemingly simple 
question led to a web of follow-ups involving COBRA eligibility, qualifying 
events, spousal coverage, and district-specific timelines. 
Using AI, I was able to cross-reference the district’s health insurance 
policy with federal COBRA laws and NYSHIP regulations. I simulated 
multiple scenarios: What happens if the retiree enrolls on their spouse’s 
plan? What if the spouse retires shortly after, does that trigger another 
qualifying event? Could these changes result in coverage gaps, and if so, 
what would the cost implications be? AI helped surface relevant guidance 
and identify potential pitfalls, which allowed me to craft accurate, 
proactive messaging for the employee. 
As with all cases, I didn’t rely on AI blindly. I uploaded excerpts from our 
district’s health insurance agreement and verified COBRA-related

Department of Financial Services. These additional reference points helped 
me validate AI's suggestions and ensure the final recommendations 
aligned with state law and district policy. 
Perhaps most importantly, AI allowed me to write clearer, more reassuring 
emails to employees facing complex, emotional decisions. I could provide 
them with timely, understandable guidance, often on the same day, which 
reduced their anxiety and built trust in our office. 
Once again, AI served as a thought partner, not a decision-maker. It gave 
me clarity, saved time, and allowed me to spend more energy on what 
mattered most: the people behind the policies.

Case Study 3: Interpreting Collective Bargaining Language and 
Drafting MOUs 
Few areas of human resources work are as significant, and as 
consequential, as interpreting collective bargaining agreements. In my 
district, we have multiple bargaining units, each with its own contract 
language that must be applied equitably and consistently. Questions about 
involuntary transfers, severance eligibility, or one-off exceptions often 
require careful analysis and precise communication. In one case, I was 
asked to write a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) to extend a 
severance benefit to a single employee, outside the standard language in 
the collective agreement. 
With the contracts already uploaded into the AI platform, I was able to 
isolate relevant sections quickly, generate sample MOU language, and 
weigh the pros and cons of similar past practices. I entered the specific 
scenario such as, years of service, bargaining unit, timeline, and intent, 
and asked for draft language that would both honor the individual’s 
contribution and protect the district from setting an unsustainable 
precedent. 
Follow-up queries included: Where else in the contract might this language 
conflict? How could we structure the benefit as a one-time exception? Was 
there a way to signal fairness without creating a new entitlement? The AI 
tool offered me a legal and linguistic head start, but I still had to adapt the 
draft with full knowledge of our district’s legal counsel, board norms, and 
labor-management history. 
It’s worth emphasizing again that AI was not a substitute for professional 
judgment. In fact, when used thoughtfully, it made me more precise. The 
AI platform would sometimes misidentify article numbers or offer language 
that needed toning for audience and context. But because I had already 
built a foundation of labor relations knowledge, AI helped me get most of 
the way to a strong solution, leaving me the space to add the human 
refinement, historical understanding, and strategic foresight that only a 
leader can provide. 
This case reinforced that when it comes to collective bargaining, AI is fast, 
helpful, and flexible, but only powerful when guided by an informed, 
principled thought leader.

Case Study 4: Navigating FMLA and Medical Leave with Empathy 
and Accuracy 
One of the more delicate aspects of human resources is managing medical 
and family leave with both empathy and legal accuracy. In one situation, 
an employee submitted a doctor's note indicating they were medically 
disabled and unable to work, but the note was dropped off in a general 
mailbox, with no formal request for leave or communication to their 
supervisor. In another case, an employee requested child care leave under 
circumstances not specifically covered by the contract. Each case brought 
not only logistical questions, but also legal, contractual, and emotional 
complexities. 
Using AI, I was able to break each scenario into its components: Does this 
qualify under the Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA)? What does the 
contract say about sick leave or disability? What’s the best way to reach 
out to the employee with clarity and compassion? The AI tool helped me 
craft non-accusatory emails, structure timelines, and clarify eligibility, 
giving me language that was both empathetic and compliant with district 
policy and federal law. 
Just as important as the answers were the follow-up conversations the AI 
helped me prepare for. What if the employee doesn’t respond? How do we 
balance compassion with accountability? What documentation is required 
to maintain compliance without overwhelming someone already facing 
medical hardship? The AI platform helped anticipate next steps and 
potential pitfalls, allowing me to remain focused on building trust, not just 
checking boxes. 
Of course, all AI-generated responses were reviewed and revised. 
Occasionally, timelines were off or assumptions about local policy didn’t 
fully align with our negotiated agreements. I regularly uploaded updated 
contract language and cross-referenced guidance from the U.S. 
Department of Labor and our district’s own legal counsel to ensure 
accuracy and fairness. 
Ultimately, this case showed how AI can be a tremendous asset in 
ensuring compliance without losing the human touch. It allowed me to 
communicate with clarity and care, ensure consistency across cases, and 
support staff with dignity during some of their most vulnerable moments.

Case Study 5: Enhancing Employee Evaluations Through Precision 
Language 
As we began the process of building a department that had seen employee 
turnover, and the loss of procedural and institutional knowledge, one of 
my priorities was to ensure that each staff member received clear, 
constructive, and personalized feedback in their evaluation. 
This wasn’t just about compliance, it was about morale, trust, and setting 
a standard for excellence moving forward. However, crafting evaluations 
that are specific, equitable, and professional for multiple employees can be 
time-consuming. That’s where AI became an essential partner. 
I used AI to help draft performance summaries for classified staff. These 
employees had overcome steep learning curves, adapted quickly to new 
expectations, and were instrumental in reestablishing structure in our HR 
and curriculum offices. I input detailed notes about their strengths, 
intelligence, attention to detail, dependability, and their ability to serve

system would suggest performance levels and justification language based 
on the lesson’s evidence, saving significant time while improving the 
clarity and alignment of the feedback. 
Just like with support staff, these evaluations were never auto-generated 
and never final without my input. I reviewed each recommendation, 
considered additional context, and made adjustments based on my direct 
knowledge of the teacher, the lesson, and our broader instructional goals. 
The AI platform simply made the process more efficient and helped me 
focus on accuracy, fairness, and professional growth. 
Whether supporting a new clerk or a tenured teacher, AI helped me 
elevate the quality of feedback I provided. Most importantly, it gave me 
time to be present, to build relationships, coach for improvement, and 
create a culture where evaluations are not just ratings, but reflections of 
care and commitment.

In my work as an Assistant Superintendent for Human Resources, AI has 
become an essential support assistant, not because it makes decisions for 
me, but because it allows me to make better decisions for others. By 
handling the repetitive, technical, and time-consuming aspects of contract 
interpretation, compliance, evaluation writing, and benefits analysis, AI 
has freed me to do what matters most, lead with empathy, build trust, and 
invest in the people who make our schools thrive. 
The examples in this chapter show what is possible when school leaders 
embrace AI not as a shortcut, but as a strategic partner. With thoughtful 
use, AI helps us be more accurate, more responsive, and more present. It

enables better leadership. I can now spend more time helping employees 
discover their strengths, understand their opportunities for growth, and 
step into leadership themselves. 
To any school leader hesitant about AI, you don’t need to be a tech expert. 
You just need to be a leader who’s willing to think critically, stay curious, 
and keep your focus where it belongs, on the people.

of the journey. At the same time, we gave students what they had long 
craved—a voice in their own learning and the chance to engage with 
content collectively, not in isolation. 
It was at this point that we knew it was time to tackle one of the most 
anxiety-inducing aspects of the classroom experience: assessment. 
Because THiNKtech already supported rich instruction, we were uniquely

Designing for the Age of AI 
As THiNKtech began gaining traction and expanding into more districts

edtech company scrambled to integrate it into their offerings. For us, it 
was an opportunity to stay true to our mission. 
Rather than allowing AI to drive instruction directly to students, we chose 
to use it in ways that enhanced the teacher’s capacity. We integrated AI 
tools to help teachers generate standards-aligned content based on their

The result was a more efficient teacher workflow, not a replacement for 
the teacher. We held fast to the belief that the most powerful learning 
moments come from human interaction. When people reflect on their most 
meaningful educational experiences, they don’t remember a website or a 
video. They remember a person. A teacher. A connection. 
AI can and will have its place. We see great promise in using it for

Chapter 7 - Engagement in a Tech-Integrated Classroom by Heather

Introduction 
This chapter is structured around three core questions related to 
engagement that educators must grapple with in the age of AI: 
1. WHAT is engagement, really? 
2. SO WHAT does engagement mean for teaching and learning in a 
world shaped by generative technology? 
3. Most importantly, NOW WHAT should we do about it? 
Each section builds on the last, defining engagement, exploring its urgency 
in the context of AI, and offering practical strategies for designing with 
both in mind. Together, they form a roadmap for educators who want to 
move from awareness to action. 
WHAT: Defining Engagement in the Age of AI 
Artificial Intelligence is reshaping education at an unprecedented pace. 
Tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, and MagicSchool AI are quickly becoming as 
ubiquitous in today’s classrooms as floppy disks, Trapper Keepers, and 
Apple IIes were for students of the late 20th century. These tools 
personalize learning, suggest revisions, analyze data, and streamline 
planning, introducing both new efficiencies and fresh pathways to support 
students. 
Yet with promise comes pressure. Educators are encouraged, or expected, 
to adopt AI, often without sufficient guidance. In this rush, a dangerous 
myth has emerged: that using AI automatically leads to student 
engagement. Let’s be clear; it doesn’t. 
AI may make things faster. It may make them novel. It may even make 
them fun. But efficiency is not engagement. Novelty is not engagement. 
Fun is not engagement. These are not bad outcomes, but they are not the 
same as authentic engagement. Confusing them will lead to missing the 
mark on both fronts, AI and engagement. 
Before connecting the dots between AI and engagement, it’s important to 
establish a clear understanding of engagement itself. Engagement is more 
than mere participation or compliance—it reflects a student’s emotional 
and cognitive investment in learning. To effectively leverage AI in the 
classroom, we must first define what engagement looks like and how it can 
be nurtured. 

83 

The Engagement Continuum 
In my first book, Engagement is Not a Unicorn (It’s a Narwhal), I 
introduced the Engagement Framework, composed of two key tools: the 
Engagement Continuum and the Engagement Matrix. These help educators 
(a) distinguish between levels of engagement and (b) understand the 
underlying motivations that drive them. 
One of the challenges of engagement is that it is not a yes/no binary. 
Engagement is actually a continuum of four levels: 

These levels help us differentiate between surface-level participation and 
deep, meaningful involvement in learning. They remind us that not all 
participation is equal, and that our goal should not be mere compliance, 
but something more. We want students to care about the work they are 
doing, not just complete it. 
Unfortunately, many schools equate student compliance with engagement. 
And while it’s natural to feel relief when a previously disengaged student 
becomes compliant, we must distinguish between growth and attainment. 
Compliance is a step on the path, but not the destination, and reflects 
extrinsic motivation, not authentic investment. Simply put, a compliant 
student is not an engaged one. That distinction matters. If we conflate the 
two, we risk designing instruction around control, not curiosity. 
This is not to say that all tasks can reach the “interested” or “absorbed” 
levels of engagement. For example, though I’ve met folks who are 
engaged in doing their taxes, I’ve never met anyone who is engaged in 
paying their taxes. 

84 

Doing and paying taxes are different tasks, and the best one can hope for 
when it comes to paying taxes is compliance: “I will do it because I have 
to, or else a negative consequence will happen.” True engagement begins 
only at the level of Interest. Both non-compliance and compliance are 
forms of disengagement. 
The Engagement Matrix 
Another common misunderstanding around engagement is that it is 
synonymous with motivation. I cannot emphasize enough that these terms 
are related but separate. Specifically: 
• Motivation is why we do what we do. 
• Engagement is how we feel about what we do. 
Going back to the example of paying taxes, we are motivated to do so 
because we don’t want to pay a fine or go to jail. These negative 
consequences are real, and they drive the behavior. But don’t confuse the 
reason for doing something with how we feel while doing it. 
The distinction between motivation and engagement also underscores the 
roles of intrinsic and extrinsic motivation. Some individuals are motivated 
by relationships, others by rewards, and still others by avoiding punitive 
consequences. The source of motivation determines where on The

When we confuse motivation with engagement, we risk focusing on bigger 
and better motivators—extrinsic levers that may do nothing to improve 
how students feel about learning itself.

While the Continuum shows the developmental stages of engagement, the 
Engagement Matrix explains the why. It plots engagement across two 
dimensions: 
• Relationship to the Task (from left to right) 
• Relationship to External Motivators (from bottom to top) 
Think of it as a grid. The position within that grid helps educators 
determine what needs to change—either the task itself or the motivational 
forces—to deepen engagement. 

At this point, a reasonable question to ask is whether or not engagement 
is just about making tasks easy? The answer is no. In fact, the highest 
level of engagement, absorption, often involves tackling challenging, 
effort-intensive tasks. What distinguishes absorbing tasks from less 
engaging ones is the reward they offer. When we are deeply engaged, the 
challenge itself becomes the reward. We’d much rather wrestle with a 
rewarding challenge than coast through a dull, easy task. True, absorption 
is internal, sustained, and rare in required courses. Everyone experiences 
absorption in something (typically these are our hobbies or things we pay 
to do), but no one is absorbed in everything. Not every task will reach this 
level, and, unfortunately, most classroom assignments never will. 

86 

However, it is reasonable to expect that 100% of students will achieve 
interest in 100% of their classes daily. What’s more, interest is a worthy 
achievement. When students are curious, collaborative, and proud of their 
work—even in the face of challenge—it’s a signal that something’s 
working. But interest has its limits. It often fades when the task ends or 
the motivator disappears. 
We’ll use the Engagement Framework to explore how AI can either support 
or sabotage authentic learning. 
The Myth of AI as a Silver Bullet 
John Hattie’s influential work, Visible Learning, provides a meta-analysis of 
over 250 influences on student achievement. Pointedly, not one of them is 
“AI.” This is not to say that AI cannot be useful, but rather that it is not 
inherently transformational. Instead, the impact of AI depends on how it is 
used to support the practices we know matter, such as formative 
assessment, feedback, metacognition, and teacher clarity. In other words, 
AI is not the end—it’s a means; AI is not the outcome, it’s a vehicle. How 
we drive it determines where our students go. Like any tool, AI reflects the 
intention of its user. It can deepen learning or distract from it. 
Yet the narrative we often hear, especially from edtech marketing and 
district-level initiatives, is that AI is a revolutionary shortcut to success. 
This “silver bullet” thinking is seductive. It allows decision-makers to 
believe that investing in AI tools is synonymous with investing in 
instructional quality. But AI doesn’t improve learning on its own—it merely 
amplifies the intent behind its use. If that intent is vague, misaligned, or 
rooted in compliance-based models of education, then AI will simply 
accelerate the wrong things. 
This is particularly problematic when the goal is engagement. If a school 
or district adopts AI tools because they think kids like tech or to “get kids 
to do the work,” it reinforces a compliance mindset. Worse, it may mask 
disengagement with flashy interfaces or automated responses. The 
student may appear productive, but are they truly thinking? Are they 
connected to the task on a meaningful level? Or are we just creating an 
illusion of engagement with algorithmic smoke and mirrors? 
This is where the Engagement Framework becomes more than a diagnostic 
tool—it becomes a critical lens for evaluating whether and how AI tools 
actually support student engagement. We can use the Engagement Matrix 
to ask: 
• Is the AI tool enhancing the relationship to the task by making 
the work more relevant, interesting, or meaningful to the learner? 

87 

• Or is it simply shifting the relationship to the external 
motivator making the task easier, faster, or more tied to grades 
or adult approval? 
If the AI tool only strengthens the latter (external motivators), it may 
move students from non-compliant to compliant, but it won’t get them to 
interest or absorption. However, if the AI helps transform the task itself, 
by enabling creativity, providing choice, supporting curiosity, or scaffolding 
deeper thinking, then it has the potential to support genuine engagement. 
The goal isn’t to automate learning, but to augment it in ways that 
empower students. 
Our job is not to master every innovation, but to lead with curiosity and 
teach students to do the same. We are preparing students for futures we 
can’t fully predict—futures where content creation, digital interaction, and 
problem-solving with AI are likely to be the norm. These students are not 
just consuming content, they want to create it. That means our 
responsibility is to guide them, not just in using technology, but in 
understanding its power and purpose. We’ll revisit this idea more deeply in 
the “Now What” section. For now, it’s enough to say this: the tools are 
already here. Our students are already immersed. The question is whether 
we will lead them, or leave them to figure it out alone. 
SO WHAT: Why This Matters in the Age of AI 
Integrating AI into classrooms requires a fundamental shift in how we 
think about challenges. Some worry that AI will remove the productive 
struggle essential to learning, making tasks too easy, and students too 
passive. But AI should not eliminate struggle; it should relocate it. 
Struggle should be cognitive, not logistical. Students should grapple with 
big ideas, not formatting. They should wrestle with how to synthesize 
sources, not where they saved their file. Used well, AI removes barriers 
that don't serve the learning, so we can double down on the ones that do. 
Think of AI like a graphing calculator. The calculator doesn’t make you 
good at math—you still need to understand the concepts, decide what 
problem to solve, and input the data correctly. It doesn’t eliminate 
thinking; it shifts the cognitive load away from performing calculations and 
toward analyzing their meaning, questioning whether the results make 
sense, why they matter, and how they connect to bigger ideas. The 
calculator allows you to do more with what you already understand. 

88 

AI functions the same way. It can boost a learner’s capacity, but it doesn’t 
substitute for clarity, purpose, or content knowledge. A student must still 
know what they’re trying to say, argue, or create. A teacher must still 
design tasks that require original thought, nuance, and synthesis, not 
replication. Without that clarity of purpose, AI becomes a shortcut. With it, 
AI becomes a bridge. 
To extend the analogy further: AI is a calculator, but not all students are 
ready to use one. A calculator is only powerful in the hands of someone 
who understands the math behind the buttons. Likewise, AI is only 
powerful in the hands of students who know what they’re trying to 
accomplish and how the tool might help them get there. This underscores 
a critical responsibility for educators: we must explicitly teach both digital 
literacy and metacognitive awareness. Students need more than access to 
AI, they need guidance on how, when, and why to use it. 
According to the Engagement Framework, a student’s behavior on a task is 
shaped by two key drivers: (1) their relationship with the task itself and 
(2) their relationship with the consequences for doing (or not doing) the 
task, and with the person assigning it. Disengagement is likely if the task 
feels meaningless or disconnected from the student’s world, if the 
relationship with the teacher is strained, or the consequences feel 
arbitrary. This is where thoughtful task design and AI can play a pivotal 
role. We can shift engagement levels by increasing relevance and 
authenticity (task design) and by helping students feel more supported 
and successful (positive consequences or strengthened relationships). AI 
can reduce barriers and personalize learning pathways, helping students 
see value in their work and trust in their learning environment. That’s how 
we move students from compliance toward genuine engagement. 
The Power of Choice and Voice 
It may sound overly simplistic to say, “Change the task and you’ll increase 
engagement.” For some, that advice may spark resistance: What if I can’t 
change the task? That’s fair. There are many tasks in and out of school 
that are non-negotiable. We all have to pay taxes, fold laundry, and mow 
the lawn. Students have to take tests, complete homework, and pass 
certain courses to advance. Sometimes, the task cannot be changed. 
But how students demonstrate their learning can often be adapted. 
Standards define what students need to know and be able to do, but 
there's usually flexibility in how they show it. Think again about taxes: the 
government doesn’t care how you file, as long as you do. You can use a 
paper form, an app, or hire an accountant. You can itemize or take the 
standard deduction. There’s choice within constraint.

The same applies to schoolwork. Even when we can’t eliminate a task, we 
can increase engagement by embedding voice and choice.

NOW WHAT: The Educator’s Role in Designing for Engagement in 
an AI World 
In the age of AI, educators can no longer afford to merely acknowledge its 
existence—we must take active responsibility for wielding it wisely. This 
responsibility comes in two parts: 
1. Students: Learning to use AI ethically and effectively 
2. Educators: Designing learning experiences that promote 
engagement, align with goals, and model thoughtful AI use. 
Today’s students want to create, connect, and express themselves 
digitally. Eve Upton-Clark writes in a 2025 Fast Company post, “Everyone 
Wants to be a Content Creator: Gen Alpha’s Dream Job? YouTuber” that a 
survey of 910 Gen Alpha students (ages 12–15) found nearly one-third 
aspire to be YouTubers, one in five want to be TikTok creators, and 19 
percent want to develop apps or video games. But even students pursuing 
more traditional career paths like engineering, education, healthcare, 
trades, or law will encounter AI as a central part of their future 
workplaces. Educators don’t need to become influencers or tech 
entrepreneurs to respond to this reality, but we do need to guide students 
in understanding how AI intersects with their aspirations. That includes 
helping them see how AI can support, not replace, their creativity, 
curiosity, and contribution in any field they choose to pursue. 

94 

Teachers must also embrace AI. Avoiding it is futile because it’s already 
embedded in our daily lives, from voice assistants to smart devices. 
Generative AI tools like ChatGPT and DALL·E are reshaping work and 
learning at an unprecedented pace. “The Rapid Adoption of Generative AI,” 
a 2024 report by Bick, Blandin, and Deming, explains that generative AI 
reached nearly 40% workplace adoption in under three years, outpacing 
even the personal computer. AI isn’t a passing trend; it’s a transformation. 
If our goal is to prepare students for the world they are inheriting, we 
cannot leave AI instruction to chance. 
Explicit instruction in AI encompasses both technical skills while nurturing 
critical thinking and ethical decision-making, which are essential for 
authentic engagement across all subject areas. Just as we teach students 
to evaluate sources in social studies, analyze data in science, or break 
down problem-solving steps in math, we must also teach them how to 
interact with AI thoughtfully and honestly. This means knowing how to ask 
better questions, iterate on ideas, evaluate AI-generated responses, and 
apply those insights meaningfully, whether they’re exploring historical 
perspectives, conducting experiments, or developing design prototypes. 
This instruction must be purposeful, ongoing, and aligned to the types of 
thinking we want students to internalize, regardless of discipline. 
When designing assignments with AI, we must ask: What standards are 
students expected to learn, and would AI help students engage more 
deeply by removing friction, or will it enable disengagement by making the 
thinking optional? These questions are vital in aligning instructional design 
not just with AI capabilities, but with the Engagement Framework’s core 
variables: feelings toward the task and feelings toward the motivators. We 
must use AI to boost those variables, not mask their absence. 
Throughout this chapter, we've explored how engagement isn’t about 
compliance, convenience, or novelty—it’s about how students feel while 
doing meaningful, challenging work. To harness AI’s potential, we must 
build our practices around authentic engagement.

When used intentionally, AI can help students feel more capable, more 
curious, and more connected to their learning. That is both our promise 
and responsibility. This shift requires a mindset change. AI can no longer 
be an optional add-on or novelty. We must design learning that 
intentionally integrates AI and supports students in developing the skills to 
use it wisely. Engagement is not something that happens to students; it’s 
intentionally designed. That design is most effective when rooted in the 
Engagement Framework: when we purposefully strengthen the student's 
relationship with the task itself and with the consequences or the person 
assigning the task. AI is most powerful when it supports those 
relationships, helping tasks feel more relevant, purposeful, or manageable, 
and helping students feel seen, supported, and respected by the people 
guiding their learning.

Learner-Centric Design and the Zone of Proximal Development 
At the core of SAY IT Labs' design philosophy is a commitment to learner-

the child’s abilities. The AI then adjusts the level of challenge in real time,

moving to guided production tasks, and ultimately toward spontaneous 
use in context. Scaffolding is provided through multiple supports—such as 
slowed-down audio models, in-game turn taking between the built-in A.I.

verbal prompts—all of which can be gradually faded as the learner 
becomes more proficient, aligning with best practices in both pedagogy 
and therapy.

intensity input and output. SAY IT Labs leverages a powerful blend of 
auditory bombardment and ballistic motor speech movements to maximize 
this window of opportunity.

target strategy or speech pattern across multiple modalities. These 
strategies are not only part of the gameplay instructions but are also 
embedded throughout the environment—spoken by characters, integrated 
into reward sequences, or looped subtly in the background. This technique 
gives the brain ample opportunity to process and internalize correct 
productions passively, building strong auditory models that prime the 
learner for accurate production.

principles that strengthen the neuromuscular pathways involved in speech 
production. These types of movements reinforce speech as a dynamic, 
embodied action rather than a static skill.

flagship titles, Stutter Stars, supports children who stutter by embedding 
evidence-based fluency techniques, mindfulness, and speech pacing into a 
richly animated galactic adventure. Developed in collaboration with leading 
fluency experts, the game features real-time speech pacing feedback, AI-
powered progress monitoring, and automatic report generation—making it 
as practical for clinicians as it is empowering for learners. 
Another suite of games targets articulation disorders, autism spectrum 
disorder, apraxia of speech, and phonemic awareness, with tasks such as 
minimal pair sorting, sound blending, segmentation, and auditory 
bombardment. Like Stutter Stars, these games utilize a multimodal, 
adaptive learning framework that combines real-time speech input, visual 
and auditory feedback, and intelligent scaffolding powered by machine 
learning. This ensures that therapeutic goals are embedded directly into 
gameplay and adjusted dynamically based on each learner’s performance. 
While the current impact of these tools is already meaningful, the potential 
for global transformation is even greater. Because the games operate on 
trained acoustic models, the core speech recognition and therapeutic logic 
can—in theory—be adapted to any language. This opens up the possibility 
of providing AI-supported speech therapy to children across linguistic and 
cultural contexts, regardless of geography or access to traditional clinical 
services. 
The main constraint lies not in the technology itself, but in resource 
availability: building accurate, fair models requires large, high-quality 
speech datasets for each language. Collecting and annotating this data, 
and training it with machine learning techniques tailored to diverse speech 
profiles, is resource-intensive. However, the architecture is designed to 
scale. With appropriate investment and international collaboration, these 
games could offer localized therapy solutions in dozens of languages—
empowering clinicians, schools, and families around the world. 
This shift—from human-dependent, one-on-one intervention to AI-
enabled, multimodal digital therapy—represents not only a technological 
innovation, but a paradigm shift in global accessibility. SAY IT Labs’ 
approach has the potential to democratize access to speech therapy, 
particularly in regions where professionals are scarce, waitlists are long, or 
services are financially out of reach. By delivering high-quality, 
personalized therapy through speech-driven video games, the model 
reimagines what care can look like—bringing therapy to the child, rather 
than the other way around.

This independence is not just a technical convenience—it represents how

As we look to the future of educational technology and AI, SAY IT Labs

Chapter 10 - AI-Driven Differentiation by Dr. Mark Beehler 
This chapter will focus on the use of artificial intelligence (AI) to assist 
classroom educators with differentiating instruction to meet the needs of 
the diverse learners present in today's classroom. 
Before we go too far, we need to have a common understanding of 
differentiated instruction and its use in the classroom. Differentiated 
instruction has been a term used in schools and the educational setting for 
several decades, but I have learned there are several understandings of 
what that looks like in daily practice. In many ways, differentiated 
instruction is a philosophy of instruction rooted in capitalizing on students’ 
interests and abilities to expand their existing understanding and skill sets. 
That philosophy contrasts with the position that all students need to learn 
in the same way and demonstrate their understanding in the same 
manner. I find there are still educators who believe students need to adapt 
to their teaching style and justify that by suggesting students need to 
develop self-discipline and perseverance. Interestingly though, many times 
those are the same educators who refuse to sit through professional 
development that doesn’t fit their learning style. 
If the true goal of teaching is to cause learning to occur, it is more 
important than ever to adjust teaching strategies for all classrooms, not 
just those with students who have disabilities. In our post-COVID 
pandemic classrooms, there's a wider range of knowledge and skills 
among students. This renders teachers who use the same teaching 
method for everyone relatively ineffective and simultaneously increases 
their frustration. 
For the context of this chapter, differentiating instruction will be defined as 
tailoring instructional strategies to meet the diverse learning needs of 
students who are present in today's classroom. Traditionally, there were 
three facets to differentiating instruction to best meet a student's needs. 
Those involve modifying or changing the content, process, and product 
that a student will use to acquire new information or skills and to 
demonstrate their learning. Recently, the learning environment in which 
the teacher sets the stage for learning to occur has been recognized as 
another process for differentiating instruction. In many of today's 
classrooms, the learning environment is constrained to specific areas of 
the classroom or specific locations within a school. In the ideal setting, we 
would be talking about learning outside the school building, but my desire 
is for this chapter to be practical and easy to use for classroom teachers. 
So, we will limit the focus on changing the learning environment. 

116 

Differentiating instruction, which research has shown will improve 
students' learning, seems like a simple enough concept. So, we have to 
ask the question: why is it not occurring on a routine basis in all 
classrooms? The answer to that is also relatively simple. Modifying a 
lesson, or several lessons within one day, to meet a specific diverse 
population of students takes time. And if there's one thing that remains 
constant in education, it is the limitation of time. 
Classroom teachers are simply expected to do more each day and must 
deal with more behavior issues and student needs than ever before. This 
can limit a teacher's ability to differentiate or personalize learning for their 
students. Additionally, there is a limit to the amount of creativity that 
teachers have. Many teacher preparation programs do not effectively 
emphasize the importance of differentiating instruction or provide a large 
repertoire of instructional strategies for teachers to use in the classroom. 
While differentiating instructional strategies for each lesson every day 
sounds logical and would benefit all students, it is nearly impractical to 
expect a teacher to do this. Until now. 
The use of AI-enhanced education tools greatly reduces the amount of 
time it takes to develop lessons that incorporate a wide range of 
instructional strategies. Additionally, AI can provide new methods for 
thinking about how content is delivered. It can prompt different processes 
for students to learn through and is very good at generating a variety of 
products a student can use to demonstrate their learning. Additionally, I 
firmly believe it can breathe new life into a teacher who may be 
approaching burnout. Teachers struggle when working with students who 
are disengaged or not growing. Using very basic and free AI tools for 
teachers can turn that around and spark a new level of creativity in a 
teacher. And the best part is–it’s incredibly fast! 

Case Study 1 
Andrew is a first-year teacher in a self-contained 15:1 classroom. He has a 
total of 12 students in 7th and 8th grade. He is responsible for the 
instruction of science and social studies and co-teaches math and ELA. 
Students in his class range from on-grade level ability to 1st grade level 
ability. In a relatively small district without any colleagues who are 
teaching content in a similar manner, he is responsible for meeting the 
needs of a very diverse classroom without a significant amount of 
guidance. 

117 

Andrew uses Chat GPT to assist in the breakdown of curriculum into 
digestible segments. He utilizes a prompt that incorporates the existing 
curriculum maps for 7th and 8th grade, the time constraints for the unit of 
study, and the ability level of his students. Once the unit is divided into 
lessons, he then uses ChatGPT to meet the ability level of each student. By 
entering specific reading passages, ChatGPT adjusts the readability of the 
passage to the ability level of each student. Andrew also meets very 
specific needs of his students by prompting ChatGPT to create passages 
that avoid the use of blends or sounds that students have an impairment 
to. ChatGPT will rewrite a passage to a specific readability that avoids “st” 
blends, so the student can successfully engage in group readings. 
Andrew also has a student with very specific religious restrictions. Andrew 
utilizes ChatGPT to create holiday-themed activities that conform to the 
restrictions of the student’s religious beliefs. This enables the student to 
remain included without compromising the student’s religion. 
In addition, being responsible for a variety of lesson subjects, Andrew uses 
ChatGPT for lesson ideas and anticipatory sets. Throughout the year, he 
will use a prompt such as, “Give me a NYS geography lesson based on the 
Western New York region for a 7th and 8th grade self-contained classroom 
with reading abilities that span from grade 1 to grade 8, focus on the 
different regions such as town, city, county, and state.” What is generated 
is a differentiated lesson plan titled, “Where Do We Live? Exploring 
Western New York.” The plan includes lesson objectives, background 
knowledge to build, materials, key vocabulary with visuals, three leveled 
reading passages, guided discussion questions, an activity, an extension 
activity, a formative assessment, supports for diverse learners, and 
suggested cities, towns, and villages to use in the lesson. All of which is 
generated within eight seconds. 
For Andrew, the use of AI to assist in the development of lessons for an 
extremely diverse and needs-intensive student population has led to a 
successful first year with students who are engaged and growing.

• Equity – Embedding responsible AI practices to minimize bias and 
ensure that technology benefits all learners. 

• Teacher Partnership – Designing tools that amplify teacher 
expertise rather than replace it, keeping pedagogy at the center. 

By combining the science of early literacy with the ethics of responsible AI, 
CELaRAI is ensuring that the next generation of reading interventions are 
not only effective but also equitable. 
Together, these initiatives at UB demonstrate the highest potential of 
artificial intelligence in education—not as a gimmick, not as a replacement 
for professionals, but as a force multiplier for educators. Their 
innovations show how AI can extend human expertise, scale early 
interventions, and ensure that every child—regardless of zip code or 
circumstance—has the opportunity to thrive. 
In an era when AI is often associated with disruption or risk, UB offers a 
different narrative: AI for social good. Their leadership reminds us that 
when technology is designed with responsibility, empathy, and purpose, it 
becomes more than a tool—it becomes a lifeline.

Conclusion: Leading into the Future 
Preparing for the AI future is not a matter of waiting for the right tool or 
the perfect moment. It is about vision, leadership, and a shared belief in 
what education can become. Schools must create the conditions where 
thoughtful experimentation is supported, innovation is celebrated, and 
students are at the center of every decision. 
This is not a task for any one superintendent, principal, or teacher alone. 
It is a collective endeavor—where the most impactful innovations will come 
from shared learning, cross-district collaboration, and a steadfast belief in 
the capacity of public education to evolve. 
The future of AI in education will not be dictated by algorithms, but by the 
courage of educators who choose to lead, adapt, and reimagine what 
learning can be.

Chapter 14 - A Call to Action - by Brian S. Graham, Ed.D 
The Time Is Now 
We stand on the precipice of a profound transformation, a moment that 
will redefine the landscape of learning for generations to come. For 
decades, we've talked about the need to personalize learning, to close 
achievement gaps, and to prepare students for a world that doesn't yet 
exist. Now, with the emergence of artificial intelligence, we have tools 
powerful enough to make those goals a reality—but only if we act with 
vision, courage, and shared purpose. 
This isn't merely about technological adoption; it's about fulfilling 
education's most enduring promise: to unlock every child's unique 
potential, to ignite curiosity, and to cultivate the citizens and innovators 
our future demands. The AI revolution in education will not be shaped by 
code, hardware, or algorithms alone. It will be forged by the vision, 
courage, and unwavering values of the leaders and educators who choose 
to engage—those who see not only what AI can do, but what it must do to 
serve equity, innovation, and human potential, fundamentally shifting the 
paradigm of learning. 
This chapter is not just a call to action; it's an invitation to become the 
pioneers ready to lead with both imagination and integrity, shaping a 
future where AI amplifies the best of human education. 

Educators as Pioneers in the AI Revolution 
Educators have always been the quintessential pioneers. Every day, they 
brave classrooms filled with uncertainty, embrace complexity, and 
unearth possibility. They adapt, they listen, they create, and they 
champion growth. Artificial intelligence, when approached with care and 
clarity, doesn't change that reality—it empowers, providing unprecedented 
tools to amplify their craft. 
To be a pioneer in this next chapter of education means: 
• Transitioning from asking "Will AI replace me?" to boldly 
question: "How can AI transform my impact and elevate my 
students' unique learning journeys?" 
• Shifting from fear of the unknown to embracing a mindset of 
courageous experimentation and continuous growth, 
recognizing that every iteration is a step forward.

• Holding tight to what matters most—the irreplaceable human

advocating fiercely for student rights, and proactively addressing 
bias, ensuring that innovation is always grounded in unwavering 
humanity. 
We must see educators not as passive recipients of edtech, but as 
indispensable architects and active co-designers of the future. 
Teachers, principals, and district leaders should demand a seat at the 
table—not just when it’s time to implement, but crucially, when tools are 
being conceptualized, developed, and refined, ensuring they meet real-
world pedagogical needs. 

Collaborating Across Districts, Sectors, and Communities 
No district, no school, no single leader can undertake this monumental 
work in isolation. The potential of AI is too vast, its evolution too swift, and 
its implications too far-reaching for any one system to navigate alone. To 
lead responsibly and effectively, we must shatter silos and forge 
unprecedented cross-sector alliances. 
This includes: 
• Inter-district collaboration: Superintendents and teacher 
leaders can ignite change by sharing pilot outcomes, 
collaboratively developing cutting-edge AI guidelines, and forming 
dynamic regional learning cohorts. We are infinitely stronger when 
we learn from, and elevate, one another. 
• Industry partnerships: Schools must proactively engage with 
edtech developers, AI researchers, and ethical oversight 
organizations—not just to purchase tools, but to strategically 
shape their very design and functionality. True co-creation, 
where educators' insights drive development, must replace top-
down adoption. 
• University alliances: Institutions of higher education bear a vital 
role in pioneering research, developing innovative curricula, 
and rigorously preparing the next generation of teachers and 
administrators to wield AI responsibly and effectively.

• Family and community voice: Transparency and trust are the

students must not only understand the why behind AI but feel

partners and advocates in this transformative journey. 
AI presents a new opportunity to break down barriers not only between 
classrooms and content areas—but between sectors, systems, and 
generations, forging coalitions rooted in the shared belief that 
education can and must boldly adapt for the profound good of all 
learners.

constantly evolving frontier, perpetually unfinished. New tools will 
emerge at breathtaking speed. Existing tools will undergo continuous 
evolution. Policies will require constant refinement. And most importantly, 
our understanding of how AI intersects with authentic learning will deepen 
with every classroom conversation, every student breakthrough, and every 
challenge we face. 
That is why continuous improvement is not optional—it is essential.

students:

especially when the answers are unclear, pushing the boundaries 
of what's possible.

truly working? What isn’t, and why? Who is being profoundly 
served—and crucially, who might inadvertently be left behind?

impressive insights, our colleagues, our students, and our 
communities remain our most trusted and invaluable sources of 
learning and growth. 
Lifelong learning is not about accumulating credentials or attending a 
single training. It’s about showing up every day ready to innovate, 
adapt, and evolve—because our students, and the future they will 
inherit, demand nothing less than our continuous commitment to 
excellence.
